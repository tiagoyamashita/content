Product Manager 101 — overview
Product management identifies valuable problems, chooses outcomes to pursue, and helps a cross-functional team learn whether its solution works. The role connects customer evidence, business strategy, design, technology, and measurement.

## Product manager vs project manager

| Product manager | Project manager |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Owns what product outcome to pursue and why | Owns how and when an initiative is delivered |
| Studies users, markets, strategy, and results | Coordinates scope, schedule, dependencies, and risks |
| Manages an ongoing product lifecycle | Manages a temporary project with a finish condition |
| Measures user and business outcomes | Measures delivery and project outcomes |

Both roles create clarity and alignment. Neither replaces engineering, design, research, marketing, sales, or operations expertise.

See [Product manager vs project manager](xii-product-manager-vs-project-manager.md) for a detailed comparison of ownership, artifacts, metrics, daily work, and organizational variations.

## Product loop

```text
Understand → Choose → Build → Measure → Learn
     ↑                                ↓
     └────────────────────────────────┘
```

The loop is not a rigid process. Discovery continues during delivery, and measurement should inform the next decision.

## Core habits

1. Start with evidence about a problem, not attachment to a feature.
2. Define the outcome and guardrails before prioritizing solutions.
3. Make strategy a set of choices, including what not to do.
4. Give teams context and constraints, then collaborate on solutions.
5. Instrument before launch and decide how results will change the plan.
6. Treat roadmaps as communication about intent, not certainty theater.

Read the notes in order, then write a case study for a product you use: identify a user problem, propose a strategy, prioritize an opportunity, define success, and design a small experiment.
