Strategy and positioning
Product strategy is a coherent set of choices about where to focus and how the product can create distinctive value. A list of features or revenue targets is not a strategy.

## Connect the layers

```text
Company direction
  → Product vision
  → Strategic diagnosis and choices
  → Outcomes and guardrails
  → Opportunities and initiatives
  → Experiments and delivery
```

Each layer should constrain the one below it. Teams need enough context to reject attractive work that does not support the chosen direction.

## Strategy questions

1. Which customer or user segment is the focus?
2. What important problem or progress do they seek?
3. Which alternatives do they use today?
4. Why can this product win or create unique value?
5. Which capabilities, channels, or business model support that advantage?
6. What will the product deliberately not prioritize?
7. What evidence would show the strategy is wrong?

## Positioning

Positioning defines how the product should be understood relative to alternatives:

```text
For [target segment]
who [important need],
[product] is a [category/frame]
that [primary value].
Unlike [main alternative],
it [credible differentiation].
```

Validate the claims. Clever language cannot compensate for an undifferentiated product or an unimportant need.

## Outcomes and guardrails

Translate strategy into a small number of outcome metrics. Pair a target metric with guardrails so optimization does not harm trust, quality, accessibility, margin, or long-term retention.

Example:

- Outcome: increase the percentage of new teams reaching first shared workflow within seven days.
- Guardrails: support contacts, error rate, and 30-day retention do not worsen.

## Strategic options

Compare options using customer value, business value, strategic fit, differentiation, feasibility, risk, and learning potential. Make assumptions visible; a weighted score does not eliminate judgment.

## Review cadence

Strategy should be stable enough to guide choices and revisited when material evidence changes: customer behavior, regulation, competition, economics, technology, or company direction. Do not rewrite it merely because a quarterly planning meeting arrived.

## Strategy test

Someone reading the strategy should know which opportunities deserve attention, which do not, and why the chosen approach has a credible advantage.
