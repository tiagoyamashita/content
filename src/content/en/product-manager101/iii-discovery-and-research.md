Discovery and research
Discovery reduces uncertainty about customers, problems, value, usability, feasibility, and business viability before or alongside larger investments.

## Begin with a learning goal

State what decision the research will inform and which assumption could invalidate the idea. “Learn what users want” is too broad. “Determine why new administrators abandon setup before inviting a teammate” is actionable.

## Evidence sources

| Source | Best for | Limitation |
|--------|----------|------------|
| Interviews | Motivations, context, language, and workarounds | Stated intent is not behavior |
| Observation | Real workflow, environment, and hidden friction | Smaller sample and more effort |
| Product analytics | Behavioral patterns and funnel changes | Shows what happened, not necessarily why |
| Support and sales | Repeated objections, pain, and buyer context | Biased toward people who contact the company |
| Surveys | Structured input across a larger sample | Wording and selection bias |
| Market research | Segments, alternatives, trends, and constraints | Broad data may not fit your users |
| Prototype tests | Comprehension, usability, and desirability signals | Prototype behavior may differ from production |

Triangulate qualitative and quantitative evidence instead of treating one source as proof.

## Interview well

- Recruit people who recently experienced the relevant situation.
- Ask about specific past behavior, not hypothetical preferences.
- Explore triggers, current workflow, alternatives, consequences, and frequency.
- Avoid pitching the solution or asking leading questions.
- Record facts, quotes, interpretations, and open questions separately.

Feature requests are clues. Ask what outcome the person sought and how they solve it today.

## Frame the opportunity

An opportunity statement should include:

```text
For [user/segment] in [situation],
[problem or unmet need] causes [meaningful consequence].
Evidence: [behavior, frequency, severity, and sources].
If improved, we expect [user outcome] and [business relevance].
```

## Assumption mapping

List assumptions about value, usability, feasibility, viability, and go-to-market. Rank them by importance and uncertainty. Test the riskiest assumption with the smallest credible method.

## Discovery is continuous

Maintain regular customer contact and review product behavior after launch. A discovery phase that ends before delivery encourages stale evidence and late surprises.

## Ethical research

Use informed consent, collect only necessary data, protect sensitive information, avoid manipulative tests, and include accessibility and underrepresented users where the product affects them.
