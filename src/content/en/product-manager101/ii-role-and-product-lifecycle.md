Role and product lifecycle
A product manager improves the quality and speed of product decisions. The role is accountable for clarity and outcomes, not personal control over every activity.

## Core responsibilities

| Responsibility | Key question |
|----------------|--------------|
| Understand | Which user and business problems are real and important? |
| Choose | Where will the product compete, and what will it not pursue? |
| Align | Do teams understand the outcome, evidence, constraints, and trade-offs? |
| Enable delivery | Are requirements and decisions clear enough for the team to proceed? |
| Measure | Did behavior and business results change as expected? |
| Learn | What evidence should change the strategy or next investment? |

## Cross-functional partnership

- **Engineering** owns technical design, implementation quality, and feasibility input.
- **Design and research** own interaction craft and research quality.
- **Marketing and sales** contribute market, positioning, acquisition, and buyer evidence.
- **Data** supports trustworthy definitions, instrumentation, and analysis.
- **Operations and support** reveal workflow constraints and recurring customer pain.
- **Leadership** sets company direction, investment boundaries, and risk appetite.

The product manager integrates these perspectives and ensures decisions have an owner. Influence comes from evidence, judgment, and trust—not from acting as everyone's manager.

## Product lifecycle

### Explore

Study users, context, market, data, and business constraints. Frame opportunities rather than collecting feature requests.

### Validate

Test the riskiest assumptions with interviews, prototypes, technical spikes, pricing tests, or small experiments.

### Build and launch

Align on outcomes and scope, collaborate through delivery, prepare go-to-market and operations, and instrument the experience.

### Grow and optimize

Improve activation, engagement, retention, reliability, monetization, or efficiency based on evidence.

### Mature or retire

Simplify, maintain, reposition, consolidate, or sunset when further investment no longer fits strategy.

## Decision boundaries

Clarify who decides product strategy, priority, scope trade-offs, design, architecture, launch readiness, pricing, and legal risk. “The PM decides everything” creates weak decisions and fragile relationships.

## Anti-patterns

- Feature-factory backlog administration without outcome ownership.
- Acting as a messenger between functions instead of building shared context.
- Treating the loudest customer or executive request as validated demand.
- Measuring output shipped while ignoring adoption and impact.
- Writing exhaustive specifications before testing risky assumptions.
