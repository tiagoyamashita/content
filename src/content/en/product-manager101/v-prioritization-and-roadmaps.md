Prioritization and roadmaps
Prioritization allocates scarce attention and capacity to the opportunities most likely to advance strategy. It is a decision process, not a formula that makes trade-offs disappear.

## Prioritize opportunities before features

Compare user problems and outcomes first. If teams rank a mixed list of bugs, ideas, executive requests, and large initiatives, the units are too inconsistent for a useful comparison.

## Decision criteria

Consider:

- user reach, frequency, severity, and unmet need;
- expected business impact and strategic fit;
- confidence and quality of evidence;
- effort, capacity, dependencies, and opportunity cost;
- technical, legal, operational, and reputational risk;
- learning value and reversibility;
- urgency caused by a real window or constraint.

RICE, impact-versus-effort, cost of delay, and similar methods structure discussion. Their scores are inputs, not objective truth.

## A practical process

1. Confirm strategy, constraints, and available capacity.
2. Normalize candidate opportunities at a comparable level.
3. Gather evidence and expose assumptions.
4. Compare options with agreed criteria.
5. Check portfolio balance and dependencies.
6. Decide, record rationale, and communicate what is not selected.
7. Revisit when evidence or constraints materially change.

Reserve explicit capacity for reliability, security, compliance, maintenance, and discovery when those investments compete with visible features.

## Outcome-based roadmaps

Prefer themes and outcomes:

| Horizon | Example |
|---------|---------|
| Now | Reduce failed onboarding for small teams |
| Next | Improve recurring workflow adoption |
| Later | Explore enterprise administration needs |

The roadmap communicates direction and sequence. The delivery plan carries detailed scope, ownership, and dates.

## Date communication

Use a confidence range or commitment level appropriate to the evidence:

- **Committed:** scope and dependencies are understood; organization is accountable for the date.
- **Targeted:** useful planning date with meaningful uncertainty.
- **Exploratory:** sequencing intent, not a delivery promise.

Explain assumptions whenever external stakeholders depend on the date.

## Saying no

State the goal behind the request, summarize the evidence, explain the trade-off, and identify alternatives or the condition that would change the decision. A backlog is not a polite graveyard; close or archive work that is not expected to be pursued.

## Roadmap anti-patterns

- Every stakeholder receives one item to avoid conflict.
- A long feature timeline is presented as certainty.
- Priority changes without recording the new evidence or trade-off.
- Already-started work wins regardless of expected value.
- Roadmap progress is reported without product outcomes.
