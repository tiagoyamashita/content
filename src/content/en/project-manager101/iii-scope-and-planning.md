Scope and planning
A useful plan connects an outcome to accepted deliverables and owned work. It is a decision model, not a promise that uncertainty has disappeared.

## Start with a project brief

Capture:

- the problem and desired outcome;
- measurable success criteria;
- sponsor, project manager, and key decision-makers;
- in-scope and out-of-scope boundaries;
- target dates, budget, policies, and other constraints;
- major assumptions, risks, and unresolved questions;
- the approval needed to continue.

## Define deliverables

Describe results that can be reviewed, not vague activity. “Run training for support staff with recorded attendance and a published playbook” is testable; “work on training” is not.

For each deliverable, record:

| Field | Purpose |
|-------|---------|
| Owner | One person accountable for completion |
| Acceptance criteria | Observable conditions for approval |
| Reviewer | Person authorized to accept it |
| Due window | Date or milestone commitment |
| Dependencies | Inputs required before completion |

## Break down the work

Use a work breakdown structure:

1. Start with deliverables.
2. Split each into manageable work packages.
3. Add enabling work such as procurement, security review, migration, training, and handover.
4. Stop when an owner can estimate and track the work reliably.

Avoid decomposing everything into tiny tasks before the near-term work is understood. Use rolling-wave planning: detail the next horizon and keep later work at a higher level.

## Estimate responsibly

- Ask the people doing the work.
- Estimate ranges when uncertainty is meaningful.
- Separate effort from elapsed duration.
- Include review, waiting, rework, and handoff time.
- Record assumptions behind important estimates.
- Add contingency based on identified uncertainty, not an arbitrary percentage hidden in every task.

## Baseline and control

Once approved, the scope, schedule, and budget baseline becomes the comparison point. It may change through an explicit decision; silently moving dates or adding work destroys accountability.

## Definition of ready

Before execution, confirm that the objective, deliverables, ownership, acceptance, major dependencies, governance, and immediate next work are understood. Unknowns may remain, but they need owners and resolution dates.
