Schedule and dependencies
A schedule explains sequence, commitments, and decision points. Dates without dependencies and capacity are wishes.

## Build the schedule

1. List deliverables and work packages.
2. Identify predecessor and successor relationships.
3. Estimate effort and duration with the owners.
4. Assign available people and other resources.
5. Calculate feasible start and finish windows.
6. Add milestones for approvals, handoffs, and releases.
7. Review the plan with the people expected to deliver it.

## Dependency types

| Dependency | Example |
|------------|---------|
| Finish-to-start | Security review finishes before production release starts |
| Start-to-start | Documentation begins when implementation begins |
| Finish-to-finish | Testing and defect resolution finish together |
| External | Vendor contract must be signed before onboarding |

Track an owner and needed-by date for every important dependency. “Waiting on another team” is a status, not a management strategy.

## Critical path

The critical path is the longest chain of dependent work that determines the earliest completion date. Delay on a critical-path activity delays the project unless the plan changes.

Shorten a constrained schedule by:

- reducing or sequencing scope;
- removing waiting and handoff delays;
- adding qualified capacity where work can be parallelized;
- fast-tracking work that can safely overlap;
- resolving high-impact decisions earlier.

Adding people does not automatically shorten specialized or tightly coupled work.

## Milestones and buffers

A milestone marks a meaningful event with zero duration: approval, contract signed, migration complete, or launch. Do not use milestones as decorative weekly markers.

Place contingency where uncertainty accumulates, especially before external commitments. Keep it visible enough to manage; hidden padding makes the schedule hard to reason about.

## Capacity and progress

Plan around realistic availability after operations, meetings, leave, and parallel initiatives. Measure progress through completed, accepted work—not percentage guesses or hours spent.

## When a date is at risk

State:

1. the forecast and confidence;
2. the cause and affected dependency;
3. the impact on the objective;
4. options with scope, cost, quality, and risk trade-offs;
5. the decision owner and deadline.
