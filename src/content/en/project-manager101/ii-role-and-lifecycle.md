Role and lifecycle
A project is a temporary effort with an objective, constraints, and a finish condition. The project manager creates the operating system that lets contributors make coordinated decisions.

## The manager's job

| Responsibility | Practical question |
|----------------|--------------------|
| Objective | What outcome must this project create? |
| Scope | What is included, excluded, and still undecided? |
| Plan | What work, sequence, people, and budget are required? |
| Coordination | Who needs what from whom, and by when? |
| Control | How will changes, risks, and decisions be handled? |
| Communication | What does each stakeholder need to know or decide? |
| Closure | Who accepts the result, owns it, and measures benefits? |

The project manager enables work; subject-matter experts remain accountable for technical, legal, design, or operational judgments in their domains.

## Five phases

### 1. Initiate

Confirm the sponsor, problem, intended outcome, high-level constraints, and authority to proceed. Produce a short project brief rather than a detailed schedule built on assumptions.

### 2. Plan

Break the objective into deliverables and work, identify owners and dependencies, estimate effort, establish milestones, and agree how changes and risks will be governed.

### 3. Execute

Coordinate contributors, remove blockers, maintain shared context, and keep decisions moving. Execution and monitoring happen together.

### 4. Deliver

Validate acceptance criteria, prepare operations and users, complete handover, and obtain formal acceptance where appropriate.

### 5. Close

Release temporary resources, archive decisions, reconcile budget, capture lessons, and schedule a benefits review if outcomes appear after launch.

## Constraints

Scope, time, cost, quality, risk, and available people influence one another. A deadline change may require reduced scope, more capacity, or accepted risk. Present trade-offs explicitly instead of promising all constraints independently.

## Common failure modes

- Starting without an accountable sponsor or measurable objective.
- Treating a task list as a complete plan.
- Reporting activity instead of progress toward acceptance.
- Hiding uncertainty until a milestone is missed.
- Closing at launch without ownership, support, or outcome review.
